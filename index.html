What Suturing Taught Me About Attention

Draft

The first time I closed an incision on my own, I understood why surgeons talk about suturing like a meditation instead of a task.

The first time I closed an incision on my own, in a small operating theater in Gujarat, I understood why surgeons talk about suturing like a meditation instead of a task. Nobody had told me it would be slow. Every pass of the needle had to account for tension, tissue type, and how the skin would pull as it healed weeks after I was gone. There was no room to think about the next stitch until the current one was right.

I think about that a lot in lecture halls now, where attention gets pulled in six directions and rewarded for speed. Surgery punishes that instinct almost immediately. You either learn to be present for one small motion at a time, or you leave a scar that tells on you later.

Notes from a Rural Endocrinology Clinic

Draft

In Manila, I watched physicians manage diabetes and thyroid disease with a fraction of the diagnostic tools I'd assumed were standard.

In a high-volume endocrinology clinic outside Manila, I watched physicians manage diabetes and thyroid disease with a fraction of the diagnostic tools I had assumed were standard. No same-day imaging. Labs that took days instead of hours. And yet the clinicians there were faster and more confident in their reasoning than almost anyone I'd shadowed before, because they had to be.

It reframed something for me about what "resource-limited" actually means. The limitation wasn't in the medicine. It was in the infrastructure around it. The clinical thinking was, if anything, sharper for the constraint.

The Space Between a Diagnosis and a Person

Draft

Taking a patient history in the ED is never really about the history. It's about the ten seconds before someone decides to trust you.

Taking a patient history in the emergency department is never really about the history. The facts come out fine, eventually, whether or not you're good at your job. What varies is the ten seconds before that, when someone decides whether to trust a stranger holding a clipboard with information about their body they haven't even told their family yet.

I used to think good clinical communication meant asking the right questions in the right order. I'm starting to think it mostly means noticing when a patient is deciding whether you're safe, and slowing down for exactly that long.
